Research

Trail clearing behaviour in Atta leaf-cutter ants

Leaf-cutter ants, unlike most other ant species, construct cleared foraging trails: they remove all undergrowth and flatten the underlying soil. While this requires a considerable amount of time and energetic investment, workers gain greatly increased movement and resource transport speed.

In the field, some trails remain uncleared. How is the decision for trail clearing made? What individual behaviour leads to the colony-level phenomenon of cleared trails?

For my PhD project, I investigate the energetics, control mechanism, and evolutionary trajectory of the trail clearing behaviour. I use image processing and recognition methods to extract data from experiment recordings, and build systemic and agent-based models of the trail clearing behaviour.

Collective Nest construction and chain formation in the weaver ant Oecophylla smaragdina

Weaver ants are an arboreal ant species, living inside nests made up from living leaves. Construction of these nests requires the coordinated effort of a large workforce, to fold and then weave leaves into shape.

Without a blueprint, knowledge of the entire environment, or sophisticated communication, how do individuals collectively make a decision for a new nest site? And what are the dynamics of leaf folding?

We find that shape and flexibility are the key criteria for the selection of a suitable nest substrate. Workers persist longer in attempts to roll tips of leaves, and a larger workforce aggregates there over time.
