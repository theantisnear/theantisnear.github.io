Research

Trail clearing behaviour in Atta leaf-cutter ants

Leaf-cutter ants, unlike most other ant species, construct cleared foraging trails: they remove all undergrowth and flatten the underlying soil. While this requires a considerable amount of time and energetic investment, workers gain increased movement and resource transport speed.

In the field, some trails remain uncleared. How is the decision for trail clearing made? What individual behaviour leads to the colony-level phenomenon of cleared trails?

In my PhD project, I investigated the energetics, control mechanism, and evolutionary trajectory of the trail clearing behaviour. I used image processing and recognition methods to extract data from experiment recordings, and built systemic and agent-based models of the trail clearing behaviour.
One already published finding is that these trails can indeed provide energetic benefits and therefore increase a clearing colony's fitness, but that this is dependent on size of clearing workforce and levels of obstruction in the environment.
More content to follow as results are published. For some additional coverage visit Monash Faculty of Information Technology.

Collective nest construction and chain formation in the weaver ant Oecophylla smaragdina

Weaver ants are an arboreal ant species living in nests made up from living leaves. Construction of these nests requires the coordinated effort of a large workforce, to fold and then weave leaves into shape.

Without a blueprint, knowledge of the entire environment, or sophisticated communication, how do individuals collectively make a decision for a new nest site? And what are the dynamics of leaf folding?

We find that shape and flexibility are the key criteria for the selection of a suitable nest substrate. Workers persist longer in attempts to roll tips of leaves, and a larger workforce aggregates there over time.
